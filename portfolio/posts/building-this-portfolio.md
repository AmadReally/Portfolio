# Why I Built a Terminal Portfolio

Most portfolios look the same.

A hero section. A grid of projects. A contact form at the bottom that nobody fills out.

I didn't want that.

## I wanted something that felt like *me*

I spend most of my day in a terminal. It's where I think. It's where things get real — no drag-and-drop, no abstraction layers, just you and the machine, talking directly.

So when I thought about how to show the world who I am as a developer, the answer felt obvious.

*Build the portfolio inside the terminal itself.*

Not a terminal aesthetic. A real one. Commands that run. Output that responds. A console you can actually use.

## What this portfolio is for

It's not a resume. Resumes are backwards-looking.

This is a **live signal** — a thing that tells you, right now, what I'm working on, what I care about, what I'm shipping. The GitHub data updates on every deploy. The activity log shows real commits. The blog is where I think out loud.

If you came here to evaluate me as a developer — look at the code behind this.
Every component is intentional. Every animation has a reason.

If you came here because you're curious who I am as a person — read the posts.
Stay a while.

## The honest truth

I built this because I wanted to make something I was genuinely proud of.

Not impressive-on-paper proud.
*Actually* proud. The kind where you show your friends and your hands are shaking a little because you care too much about what they think.

Building this reminded me why I started coding in the first place.

Not for a job. Not for a salary.

For the feeling of having an idea at night and waking up with it real.
